## The Schrödinger Equation

The time-development of a non-relativistic quantum state is governed by the time-dependent Schrödinger Equation:

$$i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\Psi(x, t) = \left[ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} + V(x, t) \right]\Psi(x, t)$$

Where:
* $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant ($\hbar \approx 1.054 \times 10^{-34} \text{ J}\cdot\text{s}$).
* $m$ is the mass of the particle.
* $V(x, t)$ is the potential energy function.
* $\hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} + V(x, t)$ is the Hamiltonian operator.

## Step-by-Step State Evolution Workflow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Formulate the Potential V(x)">
    Define the boundary conditions and potential energy barrier $V(x)$ (e.g., Infinite Square Well or Harmonic Oscillator).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Solve the Time-Independent Equation">
    Separate spatial variables to find stationary energy eigenfunctions $\psi_n(x)$ and eigenvalues $E_n$.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Construct the General Wavefunction">
    Superpose energy eigenstates with phase factors $e^{-i E_n t / \hbar}$.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Probability Density & Normalization

According to Max Born's statistical interpretation, the square of the magnitude of the wavefunction represents a probability density:

$$P(x, t) = |\Psi(x, t)|^2 = \Psi^*(x, t)\Psi(x, t)$$

This leads to the normalization condition, asserting that the particle must exist somewhere in space:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\Psi(x, t)|^2 \, dx = 1$$

<Callout type="tip" title="Conservation of Probability">
Due to the hermiticity of the Hamiltonian operator $\hat{H} = \hat{H}^\dagger$, the norm $\int |\Psi|^2 dx$ remains constant in time.
</Callout>

## Deep Dive: Hermitian Operators

<Accordion title="Why must physical observables be represented by Hermitian operators?">
In quantum mechanics, measurable physical quantities (energy, momentum, position) are eigenvalues of operators. Hermitian operators ($\hat{A} = \hat{A}^\dagger$) guarantee:
1. All eigenvalues are real numbers $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$.
2. Eigenstates belonging to different eigenvalues are orthogonal.
</Accordion>
