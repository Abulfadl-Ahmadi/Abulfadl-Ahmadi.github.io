## Vacuum Technology

### Vacuum Generation

Vacuum systems are categorized by pressure range:

| Regime | Pressure Range | Typical Pump |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| Rough Vacuum | $10^3$ – $1 \, \text{Pa}$ | Rotary vane |
| Medium Vacuum | $1$ – $10^{-3} \, \text{Pa}$ | Turbomolecular |
| High Vacuum | $10^{-3}$ – $10^{-7} \, \text{Pa}$ | Diffusion / Turbo |
| Ultra-High Vacuum | $< 10^{-7} \, \text{Pa}$ | Ion pump + Ti sublimation |

### Pressure Measurement

The mean free path of gas molecules relates to pressure:

$$ \lambda = \frac{k_B T}{\sqrt{2} \pi d^2 P} $$

where $d$ is the molecular diameter and $P$ is pressure.

## Cryogenics

### Gas Liquefaction

The Joule-Thomson coefficient determines whether a gas cools or heats upon expansion:

$$ \mu_{JT} = \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_H $$

For gases below their inversion temperature, $\mu_{JT} > 0$ and expansion causes cooling.

### Superfluidity

Liquid helium-4 undergoes a phase transition at the lambda point ($T_\lambda = 2.17 \, \text{K}$), entering a superfluid state with zero viscosity.

## Thermometry

### Resistance Thermometers

The resistance of platinum follows the Callendar-Van Dusen equation:

$$ R(T) = R_0 \left[1 + A T + B T^2 + C(T-100)T^3\right] $$

for $T < 0°\text{C}$, where $R_0 = 100 \, \Omega$ for a Pt100 sensor.

## Optics & Spectroscopy

### Laser Fundamentals

Population inversion is the key condition for lasing:

$$ N_2 > N_1 $$

where $N_2$ and $N_1$ are populations of the upper and lower laser states.

### Atomic Spectra

The energy levels of hydrogen-like atoms:

$$ E_n = -\frac{13.6 \, \text{eV}}{n^2} \cdot Z^2 $$

## Particle & Photon Detection

### Photomultiplier Tubes

PMTs achieve single-photon detection through cascading secondary electron emission. The gain is:

$$ G = \delta^n $$

where $\delta$ is the secondary emission coefficient per dynode and $n$ is the number of dynode stages (typically 8–14).
